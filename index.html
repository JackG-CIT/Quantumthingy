Historical Context

Why Quantum Mechanics?

By the late 1800s, classical physics seemed complete. Then everything broke. Experiments produced results that Newton's laws and Maxwell's electromagnetism simply couldn't explain.

Three anomalies forced physicists to build an entirely new framework — what we now call quantum mechanics:

  • Blackbody radiation (solved by Planck, 1900)
  • The photoelectric effect (solved by Einstein, 1905)
  • Atomic emission spectra (explained by Bohr, 1913)
Core Idea

What Is Quantum Theory?

Quantum theory describes nature at the smallest scales — atoms, electrons, photons. Its core claim is radical:

Energy comes in discrete chunks called quanta. You cannot have half a quantum of light — you get exactly one photon or none.

This is completely unlike classical waves, which can carry any arbitrary amount of energy. Quantum mechanics replaces certainty with probability — we describe what might happen, not what will happen.

The Big Idea

The Double-Slit Experiment

This simulation runs the most famous experiment in physics. Fire electrons (or photons) through two narrow slits at a detector screen. The question: what pattern appears?

  1. In Wave mode, watch the interference pattern — bright and dark bands, exactly like water waves. This is constructive and destructive interference.
  2. Switch to Particle mode. Each dot is a single quantum, landing at a random spot. You can't predict where.
  3. Watch long enough, and the random dots build up the same interference pattern as the wave. Mind-bending.
Standard 15.1.1

Light as a Wave

When light passes through two slits, each slit acts as a new point source of waves (Huygens' principle). These circular wave fronts spread out and overlap — sometimes adding together, sometimes canceling out.

Constructive interference: Crest meets crest → bright fringe (maximum intensity)

Destructive interference: Crest meets trough → dark fringe (zero intensity)

Fringe Locations

Where Are the Bright Bands?

Bright fringes (maxima) appear where path difference = whole number of wavelengths:

Bright fringe condition
\[ d \sin\theta = m\lambda \quad (m = 0, \pm1, \pm2, \ldots) \]

Try it in the sim: increase λ (drag wavelength slider right) and watch the bright bands spread further apart. Increase d (slit separation) and they compress. This is exactly what the equation predicts.

AP Tip: The central maximum (m = 0) is always the brightest. Intensity decreases for higher-order fringes due to the single-slit envelope — not tested in AP Physics 2, but good to know.

Standard 15.1.2

Light as a Particle

Einstein's 1905 explanation of the photoelectric effect proved light also travels in discrete packets called photons. Each photon carries a fixed amount of energy tied to its frequency:

Planck-Einstein Relation
\[ E = hf = \frac{hc}{\lambda} \]
EEnergy of one photon (joules)
hPlanck's constant = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s
fFrequency of light (Hz)
cSpeed of light = 3 × 10⁸ m/s
λWavelength of light
The Born Rule

Probability, Not Certainty

In particle mode, you cannot predict exactly where the next particle lands. Quantum mechanics replaces determinism with probability distributions.

The probability of finding a particle at position y is given by the Born Rule:

Born Rule (probability density)
\[ P(y) \propto |\psi(y)|^2 \]

Here ψ (psi) is the wave function — a mathematical object that encodes all information about the quantum state. Its squared magnitude gives the probability. This is the heart of quantum mechanics.

Key Insight

Watch It Build Up

Let 1,000+ particles land in the simulation. Notice: the random hits accumulate into the interference pattern. Each individual event is random — but the statistics are governed by the wave function.

The probability distribution for a quantum particle is determined by its wave-like nature, even when the particle itself is detected as a point-like object.

Standard 15.1.3

Wave–Particle Duality

Light (and matter!) exhibits both wave-like and particle-like behavior. Which aspect you observe depends entirely on how you measure it.

🌊 WAVE BEHAVIOR

Interference, diffraction, superposition. Spread across space. No definite position.

⚛ PARTICLE BEHAVIOR

Localized hit. Discrete energy (hf). Definite position when measured.

de Broglie's Hypothesis

Matter Has Wavelength Too

In 1924, Louis de Broglie extended duality to all matter. Electrons, protons — anything with momentum — also has an associated wavelength:

de Broglie Wavelength
\[ \lambda = \frac{h}{p} = \frac{h}{mv} \]
λde Broglie wavelength of the particle
hPlanck's constant (6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s)
pMomentum of the particle
mMass of the particle
vSpeed of the particle

Why don't we see this for everyday objects? A 1 kg ball at 1 m/s has λ ≈ 6.6 × 10⁻³⁴ m — far smaller than a proton. Quantum effects are completely undetectable at human scales.

The Measurement Problem

Observation Changes Everything

If you place a detector at the slits to find out which slit the particle went through, something bizarre happens: the interference pattern disappears. The particle suddenly behaves classically.

The act of measurement forces a quantum system to "choose" a definite state. Before measurement, the particle exists in superposition — traveling through both slits simultaneously.

This is not a limitation of our instruments. It is a fundamental feature of quantum reality.

AP Exam Prep

What You Must Know

  • Light exhibits both wave and particle properties — neither model alone is complete
  • Photon energy: E = hf — higher frequency = more energy per photon
  • de Broglie wavelength: λ = h/p — faster/heavier particles have shorter wavelengths
  • The interference pattern reveals the probability distribution for particle arrival
  • Increasing slit separation compresses fringes; increasing wavelength spreads them
Common FRQ Theme

Sample AP-Style Question

Electrons are fired at a double slit with separation d = 2.0 × 10⁻⁶ m. The screen is L = 1.0 m away. The first-order bright fringe appears at y = 0.15 m. (a) Find the de Broglie wavelength of the electrons. (b) Find their momentum. (c) What happens to the fringe spacing if the electrons are accelerated to higher speed?

Approach:

  1. Use small angle: sin θ ≈ tan θ = y/L → θ = 0.15 rad
  2. Bright fringe: d sin θ = mλ → λ = d·y/L = (2×10⁻⁶)(0.15)/1.0 = 3×10⁻⁷ m
  3. Momentum: p = h/λ = 6.626×10⁻³⁴ / 3×10⁻⁷ ≈ 2.2×10⁻²⁷ kg·m/s
  4. Higher speed → larger p → smaller λ → fringes compress (spacing decreases)
Misconceptions to Avoid

Don't Fall for These

  • ❌ "Wave OR particle" — It's always both. The behavior depends on what you measure.
  • ❌ "Higher frequency = longer wavelength" — The opposite: c = fλ, so they're inversely related.
  • ❌ "Larger slit separation spreads fringes" — It compresses them. Check the equation: y ∝ λL/d.
  • ❌ "Heavier particles show bigger quantum effects" — Larger mass → larger momentum → shorter λ → less quantum behavior.

Quick Reference — AP Physics 2

Quantum Postulate

Energy is Quantized

Energy exists in discrete packets (quanta). One photon of frequency f carries exactly E = hf joules. You can't have half a photon.

Planck's Constant

h = 6.626 × 10⁻³⁴ J·s

The fundamental scale of quantum mechanics. Also useful as h = 4.136 × 10⁻¹⁵ eV·s when working with electron volts.

de Broglie

λ = h / mv

All matter has wave properties. Faster or heavier particles have shorter wavelengths and weaker quantum effects.

Double-Slit Maxima

d sin θ = mλ

Bright fringes at integer multiples m = 0, ±1, ±2... The central maximum (m = 0) is brightest.

Born Rule

P(y) ∝ |ψ(y)|²

The probability of detecting a particle at y is proportional to the square of the wave function amplitude at that location.
