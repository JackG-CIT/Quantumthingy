Historical Context

Why Quantum Mechanics?

Classical physics seemed complete by the late 1800s. Three experiments shattered that confidence:

  • Blackbody radiation — Planck, 1900
  • The photoelectric effect — Einstein, 1905
  • Atomic emission spectra — Bohr, 1913
Core Idea

Energy is Quantized

You cannot have half a photon. Energy comes in discrete packets. Quantum mechanics replaces certainty with probability.

Sample FRQ

AP-Style Question

Electrons: d = 2.0×10⁻⁶ m, L = 1.0 m, first fringe y = 0.15 m. Find (a) λ, (b) p, (c) effect of higher speed.

  1. sin θ ≈ y/L = 0.15
  2. λ = d·sinθ/1 = 3.0×10⁻⁷ m = 300 nm
  3. p = h/λ = 6.626×10⁻³⁴/3×10⁻⁷ ≈ 2.2×10⁻²⁷ kg·m/s

What Is the Photoelectric Effect?

Shine light on a metal — electrons fly out. The details baffled classical physicists:

  • Below threshold f₀: no electrons ever, no matter the brightness
  • Above f₀: ejection is instantaneous
  • Brighter light → more electrons (not faster)
  • Higher frequency → faster electrons (more KE)

Einstein: light = photons, each with E = hf. One photon → one electron, or nothing if hf < φ.

Angle of Incidence θ

What the Angle Slider Does

The angle slider controls the angle of incidence — the angle between the incoming photon beam and the normal (perpendicular) to the metal surface.

Physically: electrons are always ejected perpendicular to the surface (normal direction) with Kmax = hf − φ, regardless of angle. The angle changes the beam geometry but not Kmax.

AP Note: Angle of incidence does NOT appear in Kmax = hf − φ. The AP exam tests only frequency and work function.

Core AP Equation

Einstein's Photoelectric Equation

\[ K_{\max} = hf - \phi \]
KmaxMax KE of ejected electron (J or eV)
h4.136×10⁻¹⁵ eV·s (or 6.626×10⁻³⁴ J·s)
fFrequency of incident photon (Hz)
φWork function of metal (J or eV)

Ejection condition: hf ≥ φ. Below this: zero electrons, always.

Threshold Frequency

Minimum f for Ejection

Threshold frequency
\[ f_0 = \frac{\phi}{h} \]

At f = f₀: electron just escapes with Kmax = 0.

Measuring Kmax

Stopping potential
\[ K_{\max} = eV_s \]
e1.602×10⁻¹⁹ C

Shortcut: Kmax in eV = Vs in volts numerically. e.g. Kmax = 1.5 eV → Vs = 1.5 V.

Why Classical Physics Failed

Prediction vs. Reality

🌊 CLASSICAL

Any frequency works given enough intensity. Dim light needs time to build up energy.

⚛ ACTUAL

Frequency threshold is absolute. Ejection is instantaneous. Intensity only changes count.

Classical wave theory is completely wrong here. This experiment proved light is quantized.

The Key Distinction

Intensity vs. Frequency

  • Frequency: determines if ejection occurs and Kmax per electron
  • Intensity: determines electrons ejected per second (photon flux)

f = 8.0×10¹⁴ Hz, φ = 2.0 eV. (a) Kmax? (b) Vs? (c) Halve frequency — ejection?

  1. E = hf = (4.136×10⁻¹⁵)(8.0×10¹⁴) = 3.31 eV

  3. Vs = 1.31 V

Quick Reference — Photoelectric Effect

Core Equation

Kmax = hf − φ

h = 4.136×10⁻¹⁵ eV·s. Ejection only when hf ≥ φ.

Threshold Freq.

f₀ = φ / h

Below f₀: no electrons ever. At f₀: Kmax = 0 eV exactly.

Stopping Voltage

Kmax = eVs

e = 1.602×10⁻¹⁹ C. Kmax in eV equals Vs in volts.

Frequency → KE

Higher f = faster e⁻

Frequency determines Kmax. One photon ejects one electron.

Intensity → Count

Brighter = more e⁻

More photons/sec = more electrons/sec. Kmax unchanged.
